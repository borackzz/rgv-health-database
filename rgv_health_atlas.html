A young population carrying an old population's disease load

Despite a median age in the low 30s, the RGV posts adult chronic-disease rates that rival much older U.S. populations, on top of the highest uninsured rates in the country. The gradient is consistent across all four counties, heaviest in Starr (poorest, most rural). Because the population is young, age-adjusted rates run higher than crude rates for most conditions — the age-specific risk is even more elevated than the headline numbers suggest.

Mortality & diabetes complications

Chronic disease drives mortality region-wide. Heart disease is the leading cause of death; the age-adjusted heart-disease mortality rate for the Lower RGV (~183 per 100,000) is comparable to South Texas overall, while documented stroke mortality (~33 per 100,000) runs below the South Texas average. The starkest county-level signal is in diabetes complications: amputations as a share of diabetes-related hospital discharges far exceed the Texas benchmark of ~5%, rising with rurality.

Social drivers of health

The RGV's disease burden is inseparable from its structural context: deep poverty, the nation's highest uninsured rates, widespread food insecurity, and near-maximal social vulnerability. All four counties sit in the most vulnerable decile of the CDC Social Vulnerability Index, and much of the population lives in colonias — unincorporated settlements with limited infrastructure and health access.

The ‘why’ — the forces behind the Valley’s health

The numbers on this site describe a young population — median age in the low 30s — carrying the chronic-disease load of a much older one. That gap is not bad luck or personal failing. It is the predictable result of how people in the Rio Grande Valley live, work, and reach care. These are the structural forces underneath the statistics — the reasons disease here is common, caught late, and hard to control.

≈500,000Texans live in colonias — about 14% of the RGV’s population

Colonias: growth without infrastructure

Roughly half a million people live in colonias — unincorporated border settlements that grew from the 1950s onward, when developers sold cheap, often flood-prone farmland in small lots without water lines, sewer, drainage, or paved roads. In the Rio Grande Valley, colonias are home to about 14% of residents.

Infrastructure has lagged for decades. A 2015 Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas review found that fewer than half of Texas’s roughly 2,300 colonias had the full set of basic services; statewide, only about half of colonia residents have sewer service, and many rely on non-tap water. Unreliable water, distance from clinics, and substandard housing become real health costs — and they cluster in exactly the places where diabetes, uninsurance, and food insecurity run highest.

28–44%uninsured in the RGV, from all residents to working-age adults

Little coverage, few providers

The Valley has some of the highest uninsured rates in the United States. About 28% of Hidalgo County residents were uninsured in 2024; among working-age adults (18–64) — the group shown on the map — the share is higher, roughly 36–44%. Because Texas has not expanded Medicaid, many adults fall into a coverage gap: earning too much for Medicaid but unable to afford a marketplace plan.

Coverage is only half the problem; supply is the other. The region is a federally designated medically underserved area with one of the lowest physician-to-patient ratios in the state. The result is care that arrives late — diabetes and high blood pressure are often found only after they have already done damage.

>20%of Valley residents are food insecure — above the national rate

Poverty and the food environment

Deep poverty shapes daily health decisions. More than one in five Valley residents is food insecure, and the food that is cheapest and closest is usually the least healthy: fresh produce can be costly or far away, while calorie-dense packaged food is everywhere.

This is a central engine of the region’s obesity and diabetes. The Food Bank of the Rio Grande Valley distributes tens of millions of meals a year across the four counties — a measure of both the need and the community response. Food insecurity here is better understood as a feature of the local economy than as an individual failing.

≈49%of South Texas adults have bought medicine in Mexico

Care across the border

The border is part of how Valley families get care. With Mexico minutes away and insurance scarce, many residents cross for medication, dental work, and doctor visits. In one population-based South Texas study, about half of adults reported having bought medicine in Mexico, where the same drug can cost a fraction of the U.S. price.

This cross-border care is a real resource for families priced out of the U.S. system. It also splits prescriptions, records, and follow-up across two countries, complicating continuity of care — and it shows how far people will go to manage conditions they cannot afford to treat at home.

Promotoras: the Valley’s own answer

The Valley helped pioneer one of public health’s most effective ideas: the promotora, or community health worker — a trusted neighbor, usually bilingual and from the community, who connects families to care, explains conditions in plain language, and follows up where clinics cannot reach. Lower-RGV promotoras organized across programs as far back as the 1990s.

Today the model is formally trained and certified through institutions like South Texas College and UTRGV. Where trust, language, and distance are barriers, promotoras are often the most reliable bridge between the health system and the people it is meant to serve.

7.2%COPD in rural Starr & Willacy — highest in the region, despite low smoking

Land, work, and weather

Geography and labor leave their own marks. Agricultural and outdoor work expose many residents to dust, pesticides, and extreme heat — part of why chronic lung disease runs highest in the rural counties even though smoking is low. Long, hot summers and few safe places to walk or play discourage the physical activity that prevents diabetes and heart disease.

These exposures rarely show up in a single statistic, but they sit behind several of them — from COPD in Starr and Willacy to the region-wide rates of physical inactivity.

2016UTRGV welcomed the first class of its School of Medicine

A region building its own capacity

None of this is fixed in place. After generations of underinvestment, the Valley is building its own health infrastructure: UTRGV opened its School of Medicine in 2016, new NIH-funded research centers are targeting the region’s cancers and diabetes, and community programs increasingly focus on prevention and the built environment.

The burden is persistent — but because it is structural rather than inevitable, it is also changeable. Understanding the ‘why’ is the first step toward changing it.

Trajectory & data vintage

A true multi-year time series requires joining successive PLACES releases; this tool exposes the two most recent vintages — county estimates from the 2024 release (BRFSS 2022) and the live tract layer from the 2025 release (BRFSS 2023). Longitudinally, the burden has been persistent and rising: the Cureus analysis of 2012–2022 hospital data found RGV diabetes and obesity climbing throughout the decade, consistently far above national levels, with no county converging toward the U.S. mean.

Methods & data sources

County prevalence (embedded): CDC PLACES 2024 release — county-level model-based small-area estimates from BRFSS 2022, the 2020 Census, and ACS 2018–2022. Percent of adults 18+; both crude and age-adjusted with 95% CIs.

Census-tract layer (live): streamed at runtime from the CDC PLACES 2025 release tract dataset (cwsq-ngmh, crude prevalence, BRFSS 2023) and joined to Census TIGERweb 2023 tract geometry. Requires internet; tract estimates are crude only (age-adjustment is not published at tract level). If a layer can't load, the app falls back to the county view and all other panels keep working.

Chronic kidney disease is not published in the mappable PLACES measure set, so it is covered narratively (see Conditions) rather than mapped, to avoid fabricated county values.

Complications & cost: Cureus 2024 (Texas hospital-discharge data). Mortality: South Texas Health Status Review. Social drivers: ACS 2018–2022 (poverty, income), Feeding America (food insecurity, approx.), CDC SVI. Poverty/income/food-insecurity values are rounded approximations and labeled as reference. Texas & U.S. benchmark rows are rounded national/state adult estimates (BRFSS/PLACES) provided for comparison.

The "44%" figure: the widely-cited Cureus diabetes prevalence (~44%) comes from hospitalized patients, not the general population, and is not comparable to the population-prevalence estimates mapped here. It appears in the diabetes narrative for context only.
